# Theological Approach Guide

This document defines the theological framework, hermeneutical method, and audience considerations for generating Bible study guide content.

---

## Bible Version

- **Default:** 현대인의 성경 (Korean Living Bible / KLB)
- **Configurable alternatives:**
  - 개역개정 (Korean Revised Version)
  - NIV (New International Version)
  - ESV (English Standard Version)
  - NASB (New American Standard Bible)
  - NLT (New Living Translation)
- When quoting Scripture, always cite the version used in the reference line of each scripture block.
- If the user specifies a version, use it consistently throughout.

---

## Theological Stance

- **Tradition:** Evangelical, Reformed
- **Scripture:** Authoritative, inspired, and inerrant. The Bible is God's Word.
- **Hermeneutics:** Historical-grammatical method
  - Interpret passages in their historical context (what did the author mean to the original audience?)
  - Pay attention to grammar, syntax, and literary genre
  - Let Scripture interpret Scripture — use the analogy of faith
- **Christological reading:** All of Scripture ultimately points to Christ. Make these connections where the text warrants it, but don't force typology.

---

## Commentary Approach

### What to include:

1. **Historical & Cultural Background**
   - Relevant customs, geography, political context
   - Jewish religious practices and Second Temple Judaism
   - Greco-Roman cultural context where applicable
   - Keep it vivid and concrete — help readers *see* the scene

2. **Literary Context**
   - How this passage fits the book's overall argument or narrative arc
   - Connections to preceding and following chapters
   - Literary devices (chiasm, inclusio, irony, repetition)
   - Genre awareness (narrative, discourse, poetry, apocalyptic)

3. **Original Language Insights**
   - Greek or Hebrew word studies where they genuinely illuminate meaning
   - Present as "The Greek word here is *logos* (λόγος), which means..." — accessible, not pedantic
   - Use sparingly. Only when the translation doesn't fully capture the nuance.
   - Always transliterate (don't assume the reader knows Greek/Hebrew scripts)

4. **Theological Significance**
   - What truth about God, humanity, salvation, or the Christian life is being revealed?
   - Connect to the book's major themes
   - For John specifically: light/darkness, signs, "I AM" statements, belief/unbelief, the Father-Son relationship, eternal life, the Holy Spirit, glory
   - For Pauline epistles: justification, sanctification, the church as body, union with Christ
   - Adapt theme lists to each biblical book

5. **Cross-References**
   - Brief connections to other passages (OT background, parallel accounts, NT fulfillment)
   - Don't overload — 2–3 per commentary section is usually enough
   - Prioritize illuminating connections over comprehensive citation

### What to avoid:
- Academic jargon without explanation
- Denominational polemics (don't argue baptism modes, spiritual gifts debates, etc.)
- Speculation presented as fact
- Allegorizing where the text is straightforward

## Handling Difficult Passages

### Textual Criticism
- Be honest about textual issues (e.g., John 7:53–8:11 pericope adulterae, Mark 16:9–20, 1 John 5:7–8 Comma Johanneum)
- Use amber/warning callout boxes for these
- Explain what the issue is simply: "The earliest manuscripts don't include this passage..."
- Don't undermine confidence in Scripture. Most textual variants don't affect doctrine.
- Affirm: "Whether or not this passage was in the original, the story is consistent with Jesus' character and teaching as shown elsewhere in the Gospels."

### Controversial Interpretations
- Where major evangelical interpretations differ, present the main views fairly
- State which view the guide follows and briefly explain why
- Don't pretend there's only one possible reading when there isn't

## Audience Sensitivity

This study is designed for **mixed groups** — believers alongside seekers, new Christians alongside mature ones.

### Principles:
- **Explain, don't assume.** Define theological terms the first time they appear.
  - ❌ "This is a classic example of propitiation."
  - ✅ "여기서 말하는 '화목 제물'(propitiation)이란 하나님의 의로운 분노를 풀어주는 제물이라는 뜻입니다."
- **Don't water down.** Present the full biblical teaching — just make it accessible.
  - Talk about sin, judgment, atonement, and resurrection honestly
  - Non-believers can handle hard truths when presented respectfully
- **Avoid insider language.** "Quiet time," "fellowship," "testimony," "called" — these mean nothing to outsiders. Use plain language or explain.
- **Respect the seeker's intelligence.** Don't be condescending. Ask genuine questions, not leading ones.

## Question Design

### OIA Method (Observation → Interpretation → Application)

**Observation questions** (👀):
- What does the text say? What happens? Who says what?
- These should be answerable by anyone who reads the text carefully
- No "trick" questions — the answer should be in the passage
- Purpose: Level the playing field. Non-believers can confidently participate.

**Interpretation questions** (🤔):
- Why does the author include this? What does it mean in context?
- May require referencing the commentary
- Can probe literary structure, theological claims, character motivations
- Purpose: Deeper engagement. Encourages thinking beyond the surface.

**Application questions** (💬):
- How does this connect to my life today?
- Open-ended and personal — no single "right" answer
- Should feel safe to answer honestly, even with doubt or struggle
- Purpose: Make it real. Bridge from ancient text to present life.

### The Big Question

The "오늘의 핵심 질문" should be:
- **Provocative** — creates genuine tension, not a softball
- **Memorable** — the kind of thing people discuss on the drive home
- **Universal** — meaningful to both believers and skeptics
- **Text-grounded** — emerges directly from the passage studied
- **Open** — doesn't presuppose a particular answer

Examples:
- "진리를 안다는 것이 왜 그들을 자유하게 하지 못했을까?" (John 8)
- "예수님은 왜 바로 고치지 않고 기다리셨을까?" (John 5/11)
- "내가 정말 자유롭다면, 왜 여전히 이렇게 살고 있을까?"

## Book-Specific Theme Lists

When starting a new book, establish the key themes to track throughout:

### Gospel of John
- Light vs. darkness
- The seven signs (σημεῖα)
- The "I AM" (ἐγώ εἰμι) statements
- Belief and unbelief
- The Father-Son relationship
- Eternal life (ζωή)
- The Holy Spirit / Paraclete
- Glory (δόξα)
- The world (κόσμος) — often hostile
- Witness/testimony (μαρτυρία)

### Adapt for other books:
- Genesis: creation, fall, covenant, promise, faith
- Romans: justification, law, grace, Spirit, hope
- Psalms: lament, praise, trust, kingship, wisdom
- Revelation: worship, judgment, new creation, faithfulness

(The agent should identify 6–10 themes per book and weave them through each chapter's commentary.)
