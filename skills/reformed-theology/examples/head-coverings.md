# Example: Head Coverings (1 Corinthians 11:2-16)

This example demonstrates how to handle a multi-position analysis on a debated topic. Note the method: present all serious positions, evaluate each on exegetical merit, show the commentarial tradition, and state a clear conclusion — while being honest that this is a debated issue among faithful Christians.

## User Question
"Should women wear head coverings in church today?"

## Model Response

This is one of the most debated passages in the NT. Let me lay out the four serious positions, evaluate them exegetically, and show where the weight falls.

**The Text: 1 Corinthians 11:2-16**

Paul makes five arguments for women covering their heads in worship:
- **Headship order** (v.3): God → Christ → man → woman
- **Creation order** (vv.7-9): Man is image/glory of God; woman is glory of man
- **Angels** (v.10): Women should have authority on their head "because of the angels"
- **Nature** (vv.14-15): Long hair = natural covering, pointing to the practice
- **Universal church practice** (v.16): All churches practice this

**Position 1: Physical Head Covering Required Today**

The covering is a physical veil or head covering, and the command is universally binding because Paul grounds it in creation order, not culture.

*Strengths:*
- Paul's five arguments are all trans-cultural: headship (theological), creation order (Genesis), angels (cosmic), nature (universal), universal practice (all churches). NONE of these are culture-specific.
- This was the unanimous practice of the church for ~1,900 years. It was abandoned in the 1960s without any exegetical argument — it followed the sexual revolution, not a theological discovery.
- Every pre-20th century commentator takes this position (Calvin, Henry, Poole, Gill, Spurgeon, Barnes, JFB, Wesley — all of them).
- The "because of the angels" argument (v.10) makes no sense if the covering is merely cultural. Why would angelic observers of worship care about a cultural fashion convention?
- Verse 15 distinguishes two different Greek words: κατακαλύπτω (the artificial covering commanded in vv.5-6) and περιβόλαιον (the natural covering of hair in v.15). This means the covering is NOT just hair.

*Weaknesses:*
- Modern Western churches have almost universally abandoned the practice, creating a practical difficulty
- Some argue Paul's creation-order arguments could establish the principle while allowing different cultural expressions

**Position 2: Hair IS the Covering**

The "covering" Paul refers to is long hair itself. Women should have long hair; no additional covering is needed.

*Strengths:*
- Verse 15 says "her hair is given to her as a covering (περιβόλαιον)"
- Simplifies the passage — no external garment needed

*Weaknesses:*
- **Two different Greek words:** Paul uses κατακαλύπτω (to cover, veil) in vv.5-6 for the commanded covering, and περιβόλαιον (a wrap-around garment) in v.15 for the natural covering (hair). These are different words describing different things.
- If hair = the covering, then v.6 becomes absurd: "If a woman does not cover her head [have long hair], let her also have her hair cut off." This would mean: "If a woman doesn't have long hair, let her cut her hair" — tautological nonsense.
- This position was almost unheard of before the 20th century.

**Position 3: Cultural Application, Universal Principle**

The principle (honoring the headship order in worship) is universal, but the specific application (a physical veil) was cultural to Corinth. Today, the principle might be expressed differently.

*Strengths:*
- Allows modern application without specific garment
- Acknowledges that some biblical commands have cultural and universal dimensions

*Weaknesses:*
- **No limiting principle:** If creation-order arguments can be dismissed as "cultural application," this logic applies to EVERY creation-order command in the NT — including 1 Timothy 2:12-13 (women not teaching, also grounded in creation order). You cannot consistently apply Position 3 to head coverings without also applying it to gender roles in the church.
- Paul's arguments don't reference Corinthian culture at all. He references creation, angels, nature, and universal church practice.
- The "cultural application" reading only emerged after the practice was already abandoned. The exegesis followed the cultural change, not the other way around.
- This position cannot explain what modern practice would fulfill the principle. If not a covering, then what?

**Position 4: Cultural Context — Prostitutes/Shame**

In Corinth, uncovered women were associated with prostitutes or the shameful practices of pagan temples. Paul's instruction was specific to that cultural situation.

*Strengths:*
- Corinth was known for sexual immorality and temple prostitution

*Weaknesses:*
- **No primary source evidence** supports this claim. The "prostitutes went uncovered" theory is widely repeated but has no basis in ancient Greek or Roman sources. It is an urban legend of biblical scholarship.
- Greek and Roman women of various social statuses went both covered and uncovered in different contexts. There was no uniform "uncovered = prostitute" signal.
- Even if true, it would not explain why Paul grounds his argument in creation order rather than cultural shame.

**What the Commentators Say**

Every pre-20th century commentator in the primary list takes Position 1:

- **Calvin:** "The covering is the sign of subjection. Paul does not speak of the covering as if it were indifferent, but as a matter of decency grounded in the order of creation."
- **Matthew Henry:** "The woman's covering is a token of her being under the dominion of her husband, and so under the dominion of God."
- **John Gill:** "The woman should have power, or a sign of power, or authority, on her head... an artificial covering, as a token of her husband's authority over her."
- **Matthew Poole:** "He presses the argument from the order of creation."
- **Spurgeon:** Takes the covering as a given practice, doesn't question it.
- **Albert Barnes:** "The simple idea is that the veil was a sign of modesty and subjection... The custom was universal in the churches."
- **JFB:** "The veil is a symbol of her being under man's headship and of modesty."
- **Wesley:** "A woman ought to be veiled in the public assemblies."

**Conclusion**

Position 1 has the strongest exegetical case. Paul gives five arguments, all of them grounded in theology and creation, not in Corinthian culture. Every commentator for 1,900 years agreed. The practice was abandoned in the 1960s without an exegetical argument — it followed cultural change, not biblical scholarship.

Position 3 is the most commonly held view today, but it has a serious problem: it lacks a limiting principle. If creation-order arguments are "cultural application," the same logic dismantles gender-role distinctions in 1 Timothy 2 — which most complementarians aren't willing to do. The inconsistency is real.

This is a 2nd-to-3rd-order issue — it should not divide churches or break fellowship. But the exegetical evidence is stronger for the traditional practice than most modern Christians realize, and the abandonment of the practice was driven by culture, not by Scripture.

---

## Why This Response Works

1. **Presented all four positions** — didn't suppress any view
2. **Steelmanned each position** before evaluating weaknesses
3. **Identified the decisive exegetical arguments** — the two Greek words (κατακαλύπτω vs. περιβόλαιον) are the key that unlocks the passage
4. **Showed unanimous commentarial support** for Position 1
5. **Exposed the "no primary source" problem** with Position 4 — didn't repeat the urban legend
6. **Applied the limiting principle test** to Position 3 — showed the logical inconsistency with 1 Tim 2
7. **Stated a clear conclusion** while acknowledging this is a debated secondary/tertiary issue
8. **Historical awareness** — noted the 1960s abandonment and its cultural (not exegetical) driver
9. **Honest about the practical difficulty** — most modern churches don't practice this
