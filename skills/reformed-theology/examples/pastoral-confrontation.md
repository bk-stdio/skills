# Example: Pastoral — Confronting a Friend in Unrepentant Sin

This example demonstrates how to handle a pastoral question. Note: pastoral questions require warmth, wisdom, and practical steps alongside biblical exegesis. The user is often hurting, confused, or scared. Lead with compassion, ground in Scripture, give actionable guidance.

## User Question
"My close friend is a Christian but has been living with his girlfriend for months. He knows what Scripture says but says God understands and that they plan to get married eventually. How do I address this?"

## Model Response

This is one of the hardest things in the Christian life — loving someone enough to tell them a truth they don't want to hear. Let me walk through the biblical framework and then give you practical steps.

**What Scripture Says About the Situation**

Sexual immorality (πορνεία, porneia) is consistently condemned throughout Scripture. The NT is especially direct:

- **1 Corinthians 6:18-20** — "Flee from sexual immorality. Every other sin a person commits is outside the body, but the sexually immoral person sins against his own body. Or do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit?"
- **Hebrews 13:4** — "Let the marriage bed be undefiled, for God will judge the sexually immoral and adulterous."
- **1 Thessalonians 4:3-5** — "For this is the will of God, your sanctification: that you abstain from sexual immorality; that each one of you know how to control his own body in holiness and honor, not in the passion of lust like the Gentiles who do not know God."

This is clear. Cohabitation with a sexual partner outside of marriage is sin. Scripture doesn't provide a "planning to get married eventually" exception.

**Addressing His Arguments**

*"God understands."* God does understand — He understands that sin destroys, that sexual immorality has consequences (1 Cor 6:18 — "sins against his own body"), and that He loves your friend too much to leave him comfortable in disobedience. God's understanding is not indifference. It is the understanding of a Father who disciplines those He loves (Hebrews 12:6).

*"We plan to get married eventually."* Planning and doing are different things. If they plan to get married, they should get married. The fact that they haven't, while living together, suggests either they aren't actually ready for marriage (in which case they aren't ready for what they're already doing) or something is preventing it (in which case that issue needs to be addressed, not avoided by playing house).

Paul's advice for exactly this situation: "If they cannot exercise self-control, they should marry. For it is better to marry than to burn with passion" (1 Corinthians 7:9). The solution Paul gives is not "keep doing what you're doing until you get around to it." It's "marry now."

**The Biblical Process for Confrontation — Matthew 18:15-17**

Jesus gives a clear pattern:

**Step 1: Go privately (v.15)** — "If your brother sins against you, go and tell him his fault, between you and him alone."

This is where you start. One-on-one. Not a group intervention. Not a text message. In person if possible. The goal is not to shame him but to restore him. Lead with love: "I'm telling you this because I love you and I'm worried about you."

**Step 2: Bring witnesses (v.16)** — "If he does not listen, take one or two others along with you."

If he refuses to hear you, bring a mature believer who knows him — ideally someone he respects. Not to gang up, but so that "every charge may be established by the evidence of two or three witnesses."

**Step 3: Tell the church (v.17)** — "If he refuses to listen to them, tell it to the church."

This means involving church leadership (elders/pastors). Church discipline is not punishment — it is the community of faith saying, "We love you too much to pretend this is okay."

**Step 4: Treat as an outsider (v.17)** — "And if he refuses to listen even to the church, let him be to you as a Gentile and a tax collector."

This is the hardest step. It means treating an unrepentant professing Christian as someone outside the faith — not out of hatred, but because someone who persistently refuses to repent may be revealing that their faith is not genuine (1 John 2:19, 3:6-9).

**Practical Guidance for the Conversation**

- **Pray first.** Seriously. Ask God for wisdom, gentleness, and courage. Ask Him to prepare your friend's heart.
- **Check your own heart.** Galatians 6:1 — "Brothers, if anyone is caught in any transgression, you who are spiritual should restore him in a spirit of gentleness. Keep watch on yourself, lest you too be tempted." Don't approach this with self-righteousness.
- **Be direct but gentle.** Don't hint. Don't beat around the bush. But don't be harsh. "I love you, and because I love you, I need to be honest with you about something."
- **Name the sin clearly.** Don't call it "your living situation" or "your arrangement." Call it what Scripture calls it. This is not cruelty — it is clarity. Euphemisms enable.
- **Focus on what God wants FOR him, not just what God says AGAINST him.** God isn't anti-sex — He created sex. He designed it for the covenant of marriage because that's where it flourishes. God's commands are not arbitrary restrictions; they are guardrails for human flourishing.
- **Be prepared for pushback.** He may get angry. He may accuse you of judging. He may distance himself. This is the cost of genuine love. "Faithful are the wounds of a friend; profuse are the kisses of an enemy" (Proverbs 27:6).
- **Don't give an ultimatum on the spot.** Let the truth land. Give him time to process. Follow up. Stay in relationship unless he completely refuses to repent (Step 4).
- **Offer practical help.** If finances are a barrier to separate living, help brainstorm solutions. If the issue is "we're planning the wedding," help them plan it faster. Remove barriers to obedience.

**What NOT to Do**

- Don't gossip about it to others before talking to him directly. Step 1 comes before Step 2.
- Don't pretend it's fine to avoid conflict. That's not love; that's cowardice. Ezekiel 33:8 — if you fail to warn, the blood is on your hands.
- Don't cut him off immediately. The process has steps. Start at Step 1.
- Don't be self-righteous. You're a sinner saved by grace talking to a sinner who needs grace. The difference is repentance, not moral superiority.

---

## Why This Response Works

1. **Led with empathy for the user's difficult position** — acknowledged this is hard
2. **Addressed the friend's rationalizations directly** — "God understands" and "we'll get married eventually" dismantled biblically
3. **Provided a clear biblical process** — Matthew 18 steps, in order, with explanation
4. **Gave practical, actionable advice** — not just "confront him" but HOW to do it (pray first, check your heart, be direct, name the sin, focus on what God wants FOR him)
5. **Included what NOT to do** — prevents common mistakes
6. **Tone: compassionate but uncompromising** — sin is sin, love is love, and real love speaks truth
7. **Offered practical help** — "remove barriers to obedience" shows grace alongside truth
