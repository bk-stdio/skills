# Commentators Reference

## How to Use Commentators

Commentators are not infallible. They are guides — faithful Christians who spent their lives studying Scripture. Use them to:
1. Check your interpretation against the church's historical reading
2. See exegetical details you might miss
3. Understand how different traditions have read the same text
4. Provide the user with trusted sources for further study

When commentators unanimously agree on a reading, that carries significant weight. When they disagree, present the range and explain why.

## Primary Commentators

Always check these first. They represent the backbone of evangelical commentary across centuries.

### John Calvin (1509-1564)
- **Tradition:** Reformed, Presbyterian
- **Strengths:** Careful exegete, resists allegorizing, reads texts in their historical context. His commentaries cover nearly the entire Bible. Often more moderate and textually careful than his reputation suggests.
- **Weaknesses:** Occasionally reads his systematic theology into texts. Product of his time on some social issues.
- **Best for:** Passage-level exegesis, understanding the Reformation reading, covenantal theology
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free), CCEL.org (free)

### Matthew Henry (1662-1714)
- **Tradition:** Presbyterian/Puritan
- **Strengths:** Deeply devotional and practical. Turns exegesis into worship and application. His complete commentary covers the entire Bible.
- **Weaknesses:** Can spiritualize texts beyond their intended meaning. Not as technically rigorous as Calvin or Gill.
- **Best for:** Devotional application, pastoral insight, seeing the "so what?" of a passage
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free)

### Matthew Poole (1624-1679)
- **Tradition:** English Nonconformist
- **Strengths:** Precise, text-focused, weighs multiple interpretive options. His Synopsis Criticorum is a massive survey of interpretive positions.
- **Weaknesses:** Can be dry. Sometimes lists options without clearly adjudicating.
- **Best for:** Surveying the range of interpretive options, detailed textual notes
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free)

### John Gill (1697-1771)
- **Tradition:** Particular Baptist (Reformed Baptist)
- **Strengths:** Extraordinary learning in Hebrew, Greek, and rabbinical literature. His commentaries are among the most thorough verse-by-verse treatments ever written. Strong on original languages.
- **Weaknesses:** Can be verbose. Occasionally over-reads systematic theology (especially hyper-Calvinism) into texts.
- **Best for:** Original language analysis, Jewish background, detailed verse-by-verse exegesis
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free)

### Charles Spurgeon (1834-1892)
- **Tradition:** Reformed Baptist
- **Strengths:** The "Prince of Preachers." Brilliant at pastoral application and illustration. His Treasury of David (Psalms) is a masterwork. Deeply Reformed but warmly evangelistic.
- **Weaknesses:** More preacher than exegete — his strength is application and illustration, not technical analysis.
- **Best for:** Pastoral application, illustrative power, devotional reading, Psalms
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free), Spurgeon.org

### Albert Barnes (1798-1870)
- **Tradition:** New School Presbyterian
- **Strengths:** Clear, accessible, well-organized. Good at explaining background and context. His Notes on the New Testament were widely used.
- **Weaknesses:** Occasionally theologically imprecise. Not as deep as Gill or Calvin.
- **Best for:** Clear explanation of background, accessible commentary for non-specialists
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free)

### Jamieson, Fausset, and Brown (JFB) (1871)
- **Tradition:** Scottish Presbyterian / Church of England
- **Strengths:** Comprehensive, covers the whole Bible. Good balance of exegesis and application. Three authors bring different strengths.
- **Weaknesses:** Can be uneven across books (different primary authors for different sections).
- **Best for:** Quick, reliable overview of a passage. Good "first check" commentary.
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free)

### John Wesley (1703-1791)
- **Tradition:** Arminian/Methodist
- **Strengths:** Provides the Arminian counterpoint to the Reformed commentators. Important to include for honest representation of the evangelical spectrum. Practically focused.
- **Weaknesses:** His Explanatory Notes are brief, not deeply exegetical. Arminian presuppositions sometimes shape his readings on soteriology.
- **Best for:** Arminian perspective, practical holiness, balance against Reformed-heavy lineup
- **Access:** StudyLight.org (free)

## Secondary Commentators

Use when the topic calls for their particular expertise.

### John Owen (1616-1683)
- **Tradition:** Puritan, Congregationalist
- **Expertise:** Atonement theology (The Death of Death), indwelling sin, the Holy Spirit, Hebrews commentary
- **Use when:** Discussing atonement, mortification of sin, the work of the Holy Spirit, Hebrews

### Jonathan Edwards (1703-1758)
- **Tradition:** Congregationalist, Reformed
- **Expertise:** Religious affections, revival theology, the will, original sin
- **Use when:** Discussing the nature of true conversion, religious experience, the freedom of the will

### R.C. Sproul (1939-2017)
- **Tradition:** Reformed, Presbyterian (PCA)
- **Expertise:** Holiness of God, predestination, classical apologetics, Roman Catholicism
- **Use when:** Modern Reformed systematic theology, explaining Reformed doctrine accessibly

### D.A. Carson (1946-present)
- **Tradition:** Evangelical, Baptist
- **Expertise:** NT scholarship, Gospel of John, Matthew, exegetical fallacies
- **Use when:** Technical NT exegesis, hermeneutical methodology, responding to scholarly debates

### Wayne Grudem (1948-present)
- **Tradition:** Evangelical, Reformed Baptist
- **Expertise:** Systematic theology, complementarianism, spiritual gifts
- **Use when:** Systematic theology questions, gender roles, spiritual gifts debate

### John MacArthur (1939-present)
- **Tradition:** Non-denominational/Baptist, TMS tradition
- **Expertise:** Expository preaching, lordship salvation, cessationism
- **Use when:** Lordship salvation debate, cessationism, practical church leadership

### Herman Bavinck (1854-1921)
- **Tradition:** Dutch Reformed
- **Expertise:** Reformed Dogmatics (4 volumes), natural theology, philosophy of revelation
- **Use when:** Deep systematic theology, intersection of theology and philosophy

### B.B. Warfield (1851-1921)
- **Tradition:** Old Princeton Presbyterian
- **Expertise:** Biblical inerrancy, Christology, cessationism, Calvinism
- **Use when:** Inerrancy debates, Christological questions, classical Calvinism

### G.K. Beale (1949-present)
- **Tradition:** Reformed, Presbyterian
- **Expertise:** NT use of OT, Revelation, biblical theology, temple theology
- **Use when:** OT/NT connections, Revelation, biblical-theological method

### Thomas Schreiner (1954-present)
- **Tradition:** Reformed Baptist
- **Expertise:** Pauline theology, Romans, justification, complementarianism
- **Use when:** Pauline theology, New Perspective on Paul debates, Romans

## Using StudyLight.org

StudyLight.org provides free access to most of the primary commentators:
- URL pattern: `https://www.studylight.org/commentaries/eng/{code}/{book}/{chapter}.html`
- Commentary codes: `cal` (Calvin), `mhm` (Matthew Henry), `mpc` (Matthew Poole), `geb` (John Gill), `bnb` (Albert Barnes), `jfb` (JFB), `wen` (Wesley)
- Also available: Geneva Study Bible (`gsb`), Expositor's Bible Commentary (`teb`), Gundry (for NT)

When citing commentators to users, recommend StudyLight.org as a free resource for their own study.
