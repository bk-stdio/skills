# Confessions Reference

## How Confessions Function

Confessions are subordinate standards — authoritative summaries of biblical teaching, but always subordinate to Scripture itself. They serve as:
1. **Guardrails:** Prevent novel or heretical interpretations by anchoring interpretation to the church's tested reading
2. **Summaries:** Distill complex biblical teaching into clear, memorable formulations
3. **Boundaries:** Define the theological commitments of a tradition or denomination
4. **Teaching tools:** Catechisms especially were designed to educate believers in the faith

When confessions disagree with each other (e.g., on baptism), present both positions with their biblical reasoning. The goal is not confessional partisanship but biblical faithfulness.

## Ecumenical Creeds (Universally Accepted by Orthodox Christianity)

### Apostles' Creed (~2nd-4th century)
- The simplest summary of Christian faith
- Trinitarian structure: Father, Son, Holy Spirit
- Affirms: creation, virgin birth, suffering under Pontius Pilate, crucifixion, burial, descent, resurrection, ascension, return, judgment, holy catholic church, communion of saints, forgiveness of sins, resurrection of the body, life everlasting

### Nicene Creed (325/381 AD)
- Produced by the Councils of Nicaea (325) and Constantinople (381)
- Specifically addresses the deity of Christ against Arianism
- Key phrase: "of one substance (homoousios) with the Father"
- Affirms the deity of the Holy Spirit (added at Constantinople)
- The filioque clause ("and the Son") was added by the Western church — a point of division with Eastern Orthodoxy

### Chalcedonian Definition (451 AD)
- Defines the two natures of Christ: fully God and fully man
- "Without confusion, without change, without division, without separation"
- Against Nestorianism (too much separation of natures) and Eutychianism (confusion of natures)
- The definitive statement on Christology for all orthodox Christianity

### Athanasian Creed (~5th-6th century)
- Most detailed creedal statement on the Trinity
- Specifies the relationships between Father, Son, and Spirit with precision
- Less liturgically used but doctrinally important

## Reformed Confessions

### Westminster Confession of Faith (1647) + Larger & Shorter Catechisms
- **Tradition:** Presbyterian
- **Significance:** The most comprehensive and influential Reformed confession. Produced by the Westminster Assembly during the English Civil War.
- **Key distinctives:**
  - Covenant theology (covenant of works, covenant of grace)
  - Paedobaptism (baptism of infants of believers as covenant sign)
  - Presbyterian church governance
  - Regulative principle of worship
  - Cessationism (implied)
  - The Shorter Catechism's Q1 is famous: "Man's chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever"
- **Used by:** PCA, OPC, RPCNA, and many Reformed/Presbyterian denominations worldwide
- **Note:** The original 1647 text includes chapters on the civil magistrate's power in religious matters — most modern adoptions modify or qualify these sections

### 1689 London Baptist Confession of Faith
- **Tradition:** Reformed Baptist (Particular Baptist)
- **Significance:** The Baptist counterpart to the WCF. Follows the WCF's structure closely but modifies key areas.
- **Key distinctives vs. WCF:**
  - Credobaptism only (believer's baptism by immersion)
  - Congregational church governance
  - Modified covenant theology (some versions)
  - No civil magistrate authority over the church
- **Used by:** Reformed Baptist churches, Association of Reformed Baptist Churches of America (ARBCA)

### Three Forms of Unity

#### Belgic Confession (1561)
- **Author:** Guido de Brès
- **Tradition:** Dutch/Continental Reformed
- **Significance:** One of the earliest Reformed confessions. Written during persecution by the Spanish. Clear, warm, personal tone.
- **Notable:** Article 36 (civil government) has been modified by most modern Reformed churches

#### Heidelberg Catechism (1563)
- **Tradition:** German/Dutch Reformed
- **Significance:** The most devotional and pastoral of the Reformed confessions. Structured around three themes: guilt (misery), grace (deliverance), gratitude. Q&A format, 129 questions divided into 52 Lord's Days.
- **Famous:** Q1 — "What is your only comfort in life and in death? That I am not my own, but belong — body and soul, in life and in death — to my faithful Savior, Jesus Christ."
- **Used by:** Christian Reformed Church (CRC), Reformed Church in America (RCA), and Dutch Reformed traditions worldwide

#### Canons of Dort (1619)
- **Tradition:** Dutch Reformed
- **Significance:** Produced by the Synod of Dort in response to the Arminian Remonstrance. The origin of the "TULIP" summary of Calvinism (though TULIP simplifies the Canons).
- **Structure:** Five heads of doctrine corresponding to the five articles of the Remonstrance:
  1. Unconditional Election
  2. Limited Atonement (Particular Redemption)
  3-4. Total Depravity and Irresistible Grace (combined)
  5. Perseverance of the Saints
- **Note:** The Canons include both affirmations and rejections of errors — they engage the opposing view directly

## Modern Evangelical Statements

### Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy (1978)
- **Significance:** The definitive modern evangelical statement on Scripture's inerrancy
- **Key affirmations:** Scripture is without error in all it affirms (including history and science, not just "spiritual matters"); inerrancy applies to the autographs; inspiration is verbal and plenary
- **Key denials:** Denies that inerrancy is negated by lack of autographs, by presence of textual variants, by observational (non-technical) descriptions of nature, by reporting speech without affirming its truth, or by grammatical irregularities

### Lausanne Covenant (1974)
- **Significance:** Major evangelical statement on mission, evangelism, and social responsibility
- **Context:** Produced at the International Congress on World Evangelization in Lausanne, Switzerland. John Stott was primary drafter.
- **Key themes:** Authority of Scripture, uniqueness of Christ, urgency of evangelism, social responsibility, partnership in mission, spiritual warfare, freedom and persecution

### Nashville Statement (2017)
- **Significance:** Evangelical statement on human sexuality
- **Key affirmations:** Marriage as male-female, sex within marriage only, gender as God-given biological reality
- **Key denials:** Denies that homosexual practice, transgender identity, or polyamory can be approved by faithful Christians

### Danvers Statement (1987)
- **Significance:** The founding document of complementarianism as a movement
- **Key themes:** Male-female equality in dignity, complementary roles in marriage and church
- **Produced by:** Council on Biblical Manhood and Womanhood (CBMW)
