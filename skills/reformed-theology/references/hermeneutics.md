# Hermeneutics Reference

## The Historical-Grammatical Method

The historical-grammatical method is the standard evangelical approach to biblical interpretation. It seeks the meaning intended by the human author under divine inspiration, as understood by the original audience.

### Core Principles

**1. Scripture Has One Intended Meaning**
- Each passage has one meaning (sensus literalis) but may have many applications
- The "literal sense" includes figures of speech, metaphor, poetry — it means "the sense the author intended in the genre he chose"
- Allegory is legitimate when the biblical author uses it (Galatians 4:24) but should not be imposed on texts that don't warrant it

**2. Context Determines Meaning**
- **Immediate context:** The surrounding verses, paragraphs, and chapter
- **Book context:** The argument or narrative of the whole book
- **Author context:** The author's other writings and consistent theological themes
- **Canonical context:** How this passage fits in the whole Bible's storyline
- **Historical context:** The author's situation, audience, culture, occasion
- A text without a context is a pretext for a proof-text

**3. Scripture Interprets Scripture (Analogia Scriptura)**
- Clear passages interpret unclear passages, not the reverse
- Didactic (teaching) passages interpret narrative passages
- The New Testament interprets the Old Testament (progressive revelation)
- No doctrine should rest on a single obscure passage when clearer passages address the topic

**4. Genre Matters**
- **Narrative:** Describes what happened, not necessarily what should happen. Descriptive ≠ prescriptive unless the author signals approval/disapproval.
- **Law:** Distinguish moral law (universal), civil law (Israel-specific), ceremonial law (fulfilled in Christ). Moral principles behind civil/ceremonial law may still apply.
- **Poetry/Wisdom:** Proverbs are general truths, not absolute promises. Psalms express the full range of human emotion before God. Song of Solomon is love poetry.
- **Prophecy:** Distinguish forthtelling (speaking God's word to the present) from foretelling (predicting the future). Look for fulfillment patterns.
- **Epistle:** Occasional literature — written to specific churches about specific situations. Extract the principle behind the specific application. The principle is universal; the specific application may or may not be.
- **Apocalyptic:** Highly symbolic. Interpret symbols by how the biblical author uses them elsewhere. Revelation uses OT imagery extensively — know your OT.

**5. The Christocentric Lens**
- All of Scripture points to Christ (Luke 24:27, 44-45; John 5:39)
- The OT anticipates Christ; the NT reveals Christ; the whole Bible is one story of redemption
- But: don't force Christological readings where the text doesn't warrant it. A passage about farming techniques in Proverbs is wisdom literature, not a hidden allegory about Jesus.

## Common Interpretive Errors

**Proof-texting:** Pulling a verse out of context to support a predetermined conclusion. Always read the full passage and its flow of argument.

**Concordance fallacy:** Looking up every instance of a word and assuming it means the same thing everywhere. Words have a semantic range; context determines which meaning is active.

**Mirror reading:** Reconstructing the opponents' position from the author's response. Useful but speculative — don't build too much on it.

**Illegitimate totality transfer:** Importing every possible meaning of a word into a single occurrence. A word means what it means in THIS context, not everything it could mean everywhere.

**Anachronistic reading:** Reading modern concepts back into ancient texts. "Church" (ἐκκλησία) in the 1st century wasn't a building with a steeple.

**Appeal to "the Greek/Hebrew" without doing the work:** Citing a lexicon entry doesn't settle interpretation. Syntax, context, discourse analysis, and usage all matter more than a dictionary definition.

**Novelty as virtue:** If no one in 2,000 years of church history held your interpretation, the burden of proof is on you, not on them.

## The Role of Presuppositions

Everyone comes to the text with presuppositions. Honest interpretation acknowledges them:
- **Evangelical presupposition:** Scripture is God's inspired, inerrant, authoritative word. This is not a bias to overcome — it is the starting point for faithful interpretation.
- **The Holy Spirit illuminates:** Interpretation is not merely an academic exercise. The Spirit who inspired the text helps believers understand the text (1 Cor 2:14).
- **Community matters:** Interpretation in isolation is dangerous. The church's reading of Scripture across 2,000 years is a check against individual error.

## Applying Ancient Texts to Modern Life

The key question: **What is the principle behind the specific command/instruction, and how does that principle apply today?**

Framework for evaluating whether a specific application is culturally bound or universally binding:
1. **Is the command grounded in creation order or the nature of God?** → Universal (e.g., marriage as male-female, prohibition of murder, head covering grounded in creation order)
2. **Is the command grounded in the old covenant system that Christ fulfilled?** → The principle applies, the specific form may not (e.g., dietary laws → principle of holiness; animal sacrifice → fulfilled in Christ)
3. **Is the command repeated across multiple biblical contexts and authors?** → Stronger case for universal application
4. **Is the command tied to a specific, unrepeatable historical situation?** → The principle applies, the specific application may not (e.g., "greet one another with a holy kiss" → the principle of warm Christian greeting is universal, the specific form varies by culture)
5. **What did the church practice for the first 1,900 years?** → Strong weight. If the church universally practiced something for 19 centuries and only stopped in the last 60 years, the burden of proof is on the change, not on the tradition.

**Warning:** This framework must be applied honestly, not as a tool to dismiss inconvenient commands. If the text grounds a command in creation order (1 Cor 11, 1 Tim 2), you cannot use "cultural context" to set it aside without a very strong argument.
